\section{The Why}

\marginnote{Unlabeled data is abundant; labels are scarce and expensive.}

The deep learning revolution was built on labeled data. But labeling is expensive:
\begin{itemize}
    \item ImageNet required 14 million images and years of human annotation effort
    \item Medical imaging annotations cost \$50--500 per expert label
    \item Autonomous driving scenes demand hours of 3D labeling per frame
\end{itemize}

Meanwhile, unlabeled data is virtually unlimited: the internet holds billions of images, trillions of text tokens, and endless video.

\newthought{Self-supervised learning}\index{self-supervised learning} extracts supervision from the data itself by designing pretext tasks\index{pretext task}, auxiliary tasks whose labels come for free from data structure.

\newthought{In order to move from expensive manual annotation to learning from raw data} we have good reason to find ways to,
\begin{itemize}
    \item design auxiliary tasks whose supervision signal is inherent in the data itself.
    \item learn representations that capture semantic structure without any human labels.
    \item transfer learned features to downstream tasks where labels are scarce.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Hands On Experience}

Consider an image. Design a pretext task:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Rotate the image by $0°, 90°, 180°, 270°$. Train a classifier to predict rotation.
    \item Split into patches, shuffle them. Train to predict correct arrangement.
    \item Convert to grayscale. Train to predict original colors.
\end{enumerate}

\marginnote{The pretext task should be hard enough to require understanding image structure.}

Each task forces the model to understand image structure, including object shapes, semantics, and texture, to succeed. The learned features transfer to real tasks like classification.

\newthought{The Learning Objectives} of this lecture:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Design pretext tasks and contrastive objectives that yield transferable representations.
    \item Distinguish contrastive methods from non-contrastive and masked-prediction approaches, and identify what prevents collapse in each.
    \item Apply masked prediction for both vision and language modalities.
\end{itemize}

\section{Contrastive Learning}\index{contrastive learning}

\marginnote{Core idea: pull similar things together, push different things apart.}

\newthought{Contrastive learning} learns representations by creating positive pairs, augmented views of the same sample, and negative pairs, views of different samples, then training to distinguish positives from negatives.

\subsection{InfoNCE Loss}\index{InfoNCE}

Given an anchor $x$, positive $x^+$, and negatives $\{x^-_k\}$:

\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{InfoNCE}} = -\log \frac{\exp(\text{sim}(z, z^+) / \tau)}{\exp(\text{sim}(z, z^+) / \tau) + \sum_k \exp(\text{sim}(z, z^-_k) / \tau)}
\end{equation}

where $\text{sim}(u, v) = u \cdot v / (\|u\| \|v\|)$ is cosine similarity and $\tau$ is temperature~\cite{vanDenOord2018InfoNCE}.

\section{SimCLR}\index{SimCLR}

\marginnote{\newthought{SimCLR.} Simple Contrastive Learning of Visual Representations~\cite{Chen2020SimCLR}.}

\newthought{SimCLR} is a simple and effective contrastive framework:

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{SimCLR}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\For{each minibatch of $N$ images}
    \State Generate $2N$ augmented views (2 per image)
    \For{each positive pair $(i, j)$}
        \State $z_i = g(f(x_i))$ \Comment{Encoder + projection head}
        \State $z_j = g(f(x_j))$
        \State Treat other $2(N-1)$ views as negatives
        \State Compute InfoNCE loss
    \EndFor
\EndFor
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\newthought{Key ingredients} of SimCLR:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Strong augmentations: random crop, color jitter, Gaussian blur
    \item Projection head: MLP after encoder; discarded after training
    \item Large batch sizes: more negatives improve performance (4096+)
    \item Temperature: $\tau = 0.1$ works well
\end{enumerate}


\section{MoCo}\index{MoCo}

\marginnote{\newthought{MoCo.} Momentum Contrast~\cite{He2020MoCo}.}

SimCLR needs huge batches for enough negatives. MoCo\index{momentum encoder} uses a queue of negatives instead:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Maintain a queue of recent embeddings as negatives
    \item Use a momentum encoder, a slowly updated copy, for consistency
    \item Update: $\theta_k \gets m \theta_k + (1-m) \theta_q$ where $m = 0.999$
\end{itemize}

\newthought{The benefit} is that MoCo works with small batches of 256 and is memory-efficient.


\section{Non-Contrastive Methods}\index{non-contrastive learning}

\marginnote{Surprising finding: you don't need negatives!}

\subsection{BYOL: Bootstrap Your Own Latent}\index{BYOL}

\newthought{BYOL} learns without negative pairs~\cite{Grill2020BYOL}:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Online network: encoder $\to$ projector $\to$ predictor
    \item Target network: encoder $\to$ projector, updated via momentum
    \item Loss: MSE between online prediction and target projection
\end{enumerate}

\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{BYOL}} = \|q_\theta(z_1) - \text{sg}(z'_2)\|^2
\end{equation}

where $\text{sg}$ means stop-gradient, preventing backpropagation through the target.

\subsection{SimSiam: Simple Siamese}\index{SimSiam}

\newthought{SimSiam} is even simpler, requiring no momentum encoder~\cite{Chen2021SimSiam}:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L} = -\frac{1}{2} \left( \text{sim}(p_1, \text{sg}(z_2)) + \text{sim}(p_2, \text{sg}(z_1)) \right)
\end{equation}

\subsection{Why Don't They Collapse?}\index{representation collapse}

\marginnote{Without negatives, why doesn't everything map to a constant?}

Several hypotheses:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Asymmetry: the predictor network breaks symmetry
    \item Stop-gradient: prevents both branches from collapsing together
    \item BatchNorm: implicitly creates a contrastive signal within each batch
\end{itemize}


\section{Masked Prediction}\index{masked prediction}

\marginnote{Mask part of the input; predict the masked part.}

\subsection{BERT: Masked Language Modeling}\index{BERT}\index{masked language modeling}

\newthought{BERT} pioneered masked prediction for language~\cite{Devlin2019BERT}:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Mask 15\% of tokens randomly
    \item Of masked: 80\% [MASK], 10\% random, 10\% unchanged
    \item Train to predict original tokens
\end{enumerate}

\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{MLM}} = -\sum_{i \in \text{masked}} \log p(x_i | x_{\backslash i})
\end{equation}

\subsection{MAE: Masked Autoencoders}\index{MAE}\index{masked autoencoder}

\marginnote{\newthought{MAE.} Mask 75\% of image patches, reconstruct pixels~\cite{He2022MAE}.}

For vision:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Divide image into patches, e.g.\ 16$\times$16
    \item Mask 75\% of patches randomly
    \item Encode visible patches with a transformer
    \item Decode with mask tokens to reconstruct pixels
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Knowledge Distillation}\index{knowledge distillation}

\marginnote{\newthought{Distillation} transfers knowledge from a large teacher to a small student.}

\newthought{Knowledge distillation}\cite{HintonDistillation2015} compresses a large teacher model into a smaller student by training the student to match the teacher's soft output distributions rather than hard labels.

\newthought{Soft labels} are produced by scaling the teacher's logits with a temperature $T$:

\begin{align*}
    p_i^{(T)} &= \frac{\exp(z_i / T)}{\sum_j \exp(z_j / T)}
\end{align*}

Higher $T$ yields a softer distribution that reveals more about the teacher's learned structure. The student trains to match these soft targets, inheriting not just the teacher's predictions but the structure of its uncertainty across classes.


\subsection{Data2Vec}\index{Data2Vec}

\newthought{Data2Vec} generalises masked prediction across modalities, including vision, speech, and text, using a single framework with teacher-student distillation~\cite{Baevski2022Data2Vec}. Rather than predicting raw pixels, audio samples, or tokens, the student predicts the teacher's latent representations of masked inputs, applying the distillation idea above to self-supervised pretraining.


\section{Cross-Modal Self-Supervision}\index{CLIP}

\marginnote{\newthought{CLIP.} Contrastive Language-Image Pre-training~\cite{Radford2021CLIP}.}

\newthought{CLIP} learns joint image-text representations from web data:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Train on 400M image-caption pairs from the web
    \item Contrastive loss: match images with their captions
    \item Result: powerful zero-shot transfer to new tasks
\end{itemize}

This bridges to the next chapter on zero-shot learning.

\section{Self-Reflection and Recap}

\newthought{Self-Reflection.}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Why is the projection head discarded after training in SimCLR?
    \item What prevents collapse in non-contrastive methods like BYOL and SimSiam?
    \item How does the masking ratio in MAE affect what the model must learn?
    \item When would contrastive learning outperform masked prediction, and vice versa?
    \item How does the choice of augmentation define what the representation becomes invariant to?
    \item What failure modes arise when the pretext task is too easy or too hard?
    \item Why does cross-modal pre-training as in CLIP enable zero-shot transfer?
\end{enumerate}

\vspace{1.5em}

\newthought{Recap.}

\begin{itemize}
    \item Self-supervision creates labels from data structure; contrastive methods pull positive pairs together and push negatives apart via the InfoNCE loss.
    \item Non-contrastive methods such as BYOL and SimSiam avoid collapse through asymmetry and stop-gradient, eliminating the need for large negative batches.
    \item Masked prediction reconstructs hidden input tokens or patches, forcing the model to capture long-range dependencies; BERT applies this to language and MAE to vision.
    \item The choice of augmentation determines the invariances of the learned representation; augmentations that are too weak yield shortcut features.
\end{itemize}

\vspace{2.5em}

\marginnote{\newthought{Teaser.} Self-supervised representations are powerful, but can we use them for tasks we never trained for, with no labelled examples at all?}

\newthought{We can now learn powerful representations without labels.} But representations alone do not solve downstream tasks. When a new category or domain appears, we still need a way to bridge from pre-trained features to task performance, ideally with zero or very few labelled examples.
