\section{The Why}\index{uncertainty estimation}\index{epistemic uncertainty}\index{aleatoric uncertainty}

\marginnote{\newthought{Confidence without calibration} is the fastest route to silent failure.}

\newthought{In the previous chapter} we built a latent space whose density tells us where the training data lives. A VAE assigns high likelihood to inputs near the embeddings it has seen, and low likelihood to those far from them. That density is already an unsupervised signal of familiarity. What it does not give us is a principled way to act on the gap between familiar and unfamiliar, nor a way to separate genuinely ambiguous inputs from inputs the model has simply never seen.

\newthought{Uncertainty estimation} closes the gap.
It asks: given a model trained on finite data under specific assumptions, how reliable are its outputs for a particular input?
The answer matters wherever consequences are real, including medical diagnosis, autonomous driving, fraud detection, and manufacturing quality control.\index{deployed ML}
A model that cannot express doubt will eventually be confidently wrong, and no downstream safeguard can compensate for an uncertainty signal that was never produced.

\marginnote{\newthought{The supervisory signal} comes from the data itself: the model's own variance across forward passes, the density of training examples nearby, the reconstruction loss of an autoencoder. No labels are needed to know that an input is unfamiliar.}

\newthought{In order to move from confident predictions to calibrated ones} we have good reason to find ways to,
\begin{itemize}
    \item separate irreducible data noise from reducible model ignorance, so that each source of uncertainty triggers the right response.
    \item quantify how much a model's predictions vary across plausible parameter settings, turning a single output into a distribution over outputs.
    \item act on uncertainty estimates in practice, routing unreliable predictions to human reviewers and detecting inputs the model was never trained to handle.
\end{itemize}

\newthought{Given} a cluster of training points and three grey candidates, ask yourself a few questions before reading on. How confident should a model be about each one? Which of the three is hardest to call, and is the difficulty due to noise in the data or to gaps in the model's experience? At what point would you stop trusting the model's judgment and pass the decision to a human?

\newthought{Form groups of five.} Each member is one network in an ensemble. The group will play four short games that turn the chapter's main ideas into a physical experiment.

\marginnote{\newthought{Materials.}
A pen and a small slip of paper for each member, plus one shared sheet for the group's tally.}

\newthought{Step 1: Train.}
Agree on a one-sentence rule that maps a number to a binary label, for example ``hot if greater than the threshold, cold otherwise''. Each member privately picks their own threshold uniformly between $4$ and $6$ and writes it on a slip of paper. The slips are hidden from the rest of the group; the small disagreement between members is what makes you an ensemble of similar but not identical models.

\newthought{Step 2: In-distribution predictions.}
The instructor calls out a number well inside the range, say $3$ or $7$. Every member shouts their prediction at the same moment. Tally agreement on the shared sheet, then repeat for two more in-distribution numbers. The ensemble converges; this is the regime where its mean prediction is reliable.

\newthought{Step 3: Out-of-distribution predictions.}
Now the instructor calls a number far from anything the group trained on, say $100$ or $-50$. Every member must still answer, but pay attention to how they answer: hesitation, half jokes, contradictory shouts. Tally agreement again. The variance has jumped, even though no member has more or less training than before. This jump is epistemic uncertainty, and it is the actionable kind: it would shrink if the group were allowed to extend its training range.

\marginnote{
\begin{itemize}
    \item Which inputs were unanimous, and why?
    \item For the OOD inputs, did anyone pick a confident wrong answer? What would have stopped them?
\end{itemize}}

\newthought{Step 4: Aleatoric input.}
The instructor calls out a number sitting on the boundary, $5.0$ when the allowed thresholds were uniform on $[4, 6]$. Members will split, but extending the training range would not help; this disagreement is irreducible noise on the input itself. It is aleatoric, and the right response is not ``collect more data'' but ``communicate the ambiguity downstream''.

\newthought{Step 5: Monte Carlo dropout.}
Pick one member to be the ``input'' and the other four to be a single network. For each round, randomly close the eyes of two of the four; only the open-eyed members vote. Run five rounds with different random closures and record the votes. The variance across rounds is the MC Dropout uncertainty. Familiar inputs leave the rounds in agreement; unusual ones scatter them.

\marginnote{\newthought{One model, many sub-models.}
Step 5 turns a single network into an ensemble at test time by sampling sub-networks; Steps 2 to 4 used a real ensemble of independently trained members. Both estimate epistemic uncertainty by sampling models rather than data.}

\newthought{Step 6: Threshold for action.}
On the shared sheet, plot the agreement level against each input from Steps 2 to 5. Where would you place a threshold above which a prediction is shipped automatically and below which it is routed to a human reviewer? Now imagine the human reviewer charges fifty euros per case; does the threshold change? What if a wrong shipped answer would shut down a production line for an hour?

\newthought{The fundamental question} is whether a model can know that it does not know. A confident wrong answer is a quiet failure; a flagged unknown is a recoverable one. Everything in this chapter, from variance across dropout masks to reconstruction error from a VAE, is one way of turning the boundary of the training distribution into a usable signal.

\newthought{The Learning Objectives} of this lecture:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Distinguish aleatoric from epistemic uncertainty and explain why only the latter is reducible with more data.
    \item Apply MC Dropout and Deep Ensembles to quantify prediction uncertainty and interpret disagreement as model ignorance.
    \item Use uncertainty estimates to detect anomalies and out-of-distribution inputs, and design graceful degradation strategies that route uncertain predictions to human reviewers.
\end{itemize}

\section{Sources of Uncertainty}\index{aleatoric uncertainty}\index{epistemic uncertainty}

\marginnote{\newthought{Not all uncertainty is the same,} and the distinction determines what we can do about it.}

\newthought{Two kinds of uncertainty} arise in any model.
Aleatoric uncertainty\index{aleatoric uncertainty} is inherent noise in the data and cannot be reduced by collecting more of it.
A sensor with finite precision will always introduce measurement noise; overlapping class boundaries in the data will always produce ambiguous inputs.
No model, however powerful, can eliminate aleatoric uncertainty.

\newthought{Epistemic uncertainty}\index{epistemic uncertainty} reflects the model's ignorance and shrinks as more data arrives.
A model trained on summer temperatures has high epistemic uncertainty about winter; show it winter data and the uncertainty decreases.
Epistemic uncertainty is the actionable kind; it tells us where the model needs more evidence.

\marginnote{\newthought{A coffee analogy.} A trained barista cannot tell two identical-looking espressos apart by sight: that ambiguity is aleatoric, baked into the inputs. Show the same barista an unfamiliar single-origin and the hesitation is epistemic, an experience gap that more cups would close.}

\newthought{The practical consequence} is that these two sources call for different responses.
High aleatoric uncertainty means the problem is inherently hard at that point; the best strategy is to acknowledge the ambiguity and communicate it downstream.
High epistemic uncertainty means the model has not seen enough relevant data; the best strategy is to collect more data, or to flag the prediction as unreliable.

\newthought{The total predictive uncertainty} decomposes into the two contributions:
\begin{align*}
    \underbrace{\mathbb{V}\bigl[y \mid x\bigr]}_{\text{total}}
        &= \underbrace{\mathbb{E}_\theta\!\bigl[\mathbb{V}[y\mid x, \theta]\bigr]}_{\text{aleatoric}}
        \,+\, \underbrace{\mathbb{V}_\theta\!\bigl[\mathbb{E}[y\mid x, \theta]\bigr]}_{\text{epistemic}}
\end{align*}

The aleatoric term is the average noise level the model expects across plausible parameter settings $\theta$; the epistemic term is the disagreement among those settings about the mean prediction. The two methods that follow, MC Dropout and deep ensembles, both estimate the second term by sampling from a distribution over models.


\section{Quantifying Epistemic Uncertainty}\index{Monte Carlo Dropout}\index{deep ensembles}

\marginnote{\newthought{Knowing what you do not know} is essential for safe deployment.}

\newthought{Monte Carlo Dropout}\cite{GalGhahramani2016} estimates epistemic uncertainty by running $T$ forward passes with dropout enabled at test time. In standard inference dropout is switched off; here we keep it on, so each pass uses a different randomly thinned sub-network.\index{dropout}

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{MC Dropout for Uncertainty}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Input $x$, model with dropout, $T$ forward passes
\For{$t = 1$ to $T$}
    \State $\hat{y}_t \gets$ forward pass with dropout enabled
\EndFor
\State Mean prediction: $\bar{y} = \tfrac{1}{T} \sum_t \hat{y}_t$
\State Uncertainty: $\text{Var}(\hat{y}) = \tfrac{1}{T} \sum_t (\hat{y}_t - \bar{y})^2$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

High variance across passes indicates that the model is unsure about this input.
Each dropout mask produces a different sub-network, so the collection of forward passes approximates a posterior over model weights.
Inputs well-represented in the training data will produce consistent predictions across masks; inputs far from the training distribution will produce scattered, disagreeing predictions.

\marginnote{\newthought{Practical detail.} In PyTorch, \texttt{model.eval()} disables dropout. To run MC Dropout you instead leave the dropout layers in training mode while still freezing batch-norm statistics.}

\begin{verbatim}
def mc_dropout(model, x, T=30):
    model.eval()                          # freeze BN
    for m in model.modules():
        if isinstance(m, nn.Dropout):
            m.train()                     # keep dropout on
    preds = torch.stack([model(x) for _ in range(T)])
    return preds.mean(0), preds.var(0)
\end{verbatim}

\newthought{Deep ensembles}\index{deep ensembles}\cite{Lakshminarayanan2017} take the idea further by training $M$ independent models and aggregating their predictions:

\begin{align*}
    p(y|x) &= \frac{1}{M} \sum_{m=1}^M p_m(y|x)
\end{align*}

Disagreement among ensemble members signals uncertainty.
Unlike MC Dropout, each ensemble member is initialised and trained independently, so the diversity of predictions is driven by genuine differences in the learned representations rather than by random masking. The price is $M$ times the training cost; the payoff is sharper, better-calibrated uncertainty, with $M{=}5$ to $10$ usually enough in practice.

\section{Application: Anomaly Detection}\index{Anomaly Detection}\index{outlier detection}

\marginnote{\newthought{One direct application} of uncertainty estimation is anomaly detection: when a model assigns high uncertainty to an input, it is effectively saying that this does not look like what it was trained on.}

\newthought{Anomaly detection}\cite{Chandola2009} asks whether individual observations deviate from a learned notion of normal.
The methods in this section can be understood as different ways of measuring how uncertain the model is about an input, through distributional assumptions, through proximity in feature space, or through reconstruction fidelity.

\newthought{Three kinds of anomalies}\index{point anomaly}\index{contextual anomaly}\index{collective anomaly} appear in practice, and each calls for a different score.

\newthought{Point anomalies} are individual instances that are anomalous with respect to the rest of the data.
A single fraudulent transaction amid millions of legitimate ones is a point anomaly.

\newthought{Contextual anomalies} are instances that are anomalous only within a specific context.
A temperature of 30\textdegree{}C is normal in summer but anomalous in winter; the value itself is unremarkable, but the context makes it suspicious.

\newthought{Collective anomalies} are collections of instances that are individually normal but together form an anomalous pattern.
A sequence of login attempts from 100 different countries within one hour is an example: each login is normal, but the pattern is not.

\vspace{1.5em}
\newthought{Statistical methods}\index{Z-score}\index{GMM!anomaly detection} assume a distribution and flag low-probability points.

\marginnote{\newthought{Parametric methods} are fast and interpretable when the distributional assumption holds, brittle when it does not.}

\newthought{The Z-score} is the simplest anomaly score for univariate data:

\begin{align*}
    z_i &= \frac{x_i - \mu}{\sigma}
\end{align*}

Flag as anomalous if $|z_i| > 3$ or another chosen threshold.
The method assumes normality; heavy-tailed data will produce many false positives.

\newthought{Gaussian Mixture Model likelihood} extends the parametric idea to multimodal data.
Fit a GMM and compute the likelihood of each observation:

\begin{align*}
    p(x) &= \sum_{k=1}^K \pi_k\, \mathcal{N}(x \,|\, \mu_k, \Sigma_k)
\end{align*}

Low $p(x)$ indicates an anomaly.
The number of components $K$ must be chosen carefully; too few and normal variation is flagged, too many and anomalies are absorbed into their own component.

\newthought{Extreme Value Theory}\index{Extreme Value Theory} handles heavy-tailed distributions by modelling the tail explicitly via the Generalised Pareto Distribution:

\begin{align*}
    F(x) &= 1 - \left(1 + \frac{\xi\, x}{\sigma}\right)^{-1/\xi}
\end{align*}


\vspace{1.5em}
\newthought{Proximity-based methods}\index{k-NN distance}\index{Local Outlier Factor} make precise the intuition that anomalies are far from their neighbours, with density either ignored or compensated for.

\marginnote{\newthought{Distance and density.} k-NN ranks by distance alone; LOF asks whether the local density looks like that of the surroundings.}

\newthought{$k$-NN distance} assigns each point an anomaly score equal to its distance to its $k$-th nearest neighbour:

\begin{align*}
    \text{score}(x) &= d(x,\, x^{(k)})
\end{align*}

Points far from their neighbours score high.
The method is simple and effective but sensitive to the choice of $k$ and to varying cluster densities.

\newthought{Local Outlier Factor}\cite{Breunig2000} accounts for varying density by comparing each point's local reachability density to that of its neighbours:

\begin{align*}
    \text{LOF}_k(x) &= \frac{1}{|N_k(x)|} \sum_{y \in N_k(x)} \frac{\text{lrd}_k(y)}{\text{lrd}_k(x)}
\end{align*}

where $\text{lrd}_k(x)$ is the local reachability density.
LOF $\approx 1$ means the point sits at a density similar to its neighbours; LOF $\gg 1$ means it sits in a sparser region and is likely anomalous.

\vspace{1.5em}
\newthought{Reconstruction-based methods}\index{reconstruction error}\index{VAE!anomaly detection} use a generative model trained on normal data and turn its reconstruction loss into an anomaly score. This is exactly the bridge from the previous chapter: a VAE that places an input far from its prior, or that reconstructs it poorly, is telling us the input does not belong.

\marginnote{\newthought{If the model cannot reconstruct it,} it has probably never seen anything like it.}

\newthought{Autoencoder reconstruction error} provides a natural anomaly score.
Train an autoencoder on normal data only, then score each test point by how poorly it reconstructs:

\begin{align*}
    \text{score}(x) &= \|x - \text{decode}(\text{encode}(x))\|^2
\end{align*}

High reconstruction error indicates the model has not learned to represent this type of input.

\newthought{VAE log-likelihood} refines the idea by using the negative ELBO as the anomaly score:

\begin{align*}
    \text{score}(x) &= -\text{ELBO}(x) \\
                     &= -\mathbb{E}_{q(z|x)}[\log p(x|z)] + D_{\text{KL}}\!\bigl(q(z|x) \,\|\, p(z)\bigr)
\end{align*}

An anomalous input will both reconstruct poorly and require an unusual latent encoding, contributing to both terms.


\section{Application: Out-of-Distribution Detection}\index{OOD detection}\index{softmax confidence}

\marginnote{\newthought{OOD detection} formalises the question: is this input from the training distribution at all?}

\newthought{Where anomaly detection} asks whether a point deviates from the training distribution, out-of-distribution detection frames this as a binary decision problem: in-distribution or out.
The distinction matters because OOD methods are designed for the regime where the input is so far from the training data that the model's learned representations may be entirely unreliable.

\newthought{Softmax confidence} is the naive baseline: flag inputs where $\max_k\, p(y{=}k|x)$ is low.
The problem is that neural networks are often confidently wrong on OOD data; the softmax normalises to sum to one regardless of how far the input is from any training example.

\newthought{ODIN}\index{ODIN}\cite{LiangODIN2018} improves on softmax confidence by combining temperature scaling with input perturbation:

\begin{align*}
    \tilde{x} &= x - \epsilon \cdot \text{sign}\!\bigl(\nabla_x \log p(y{=}\hat{y}|x;\, T)\bigr)
\end{align*}

\newthought{Energy-based OOD detection}\index{energy score}\cite{LiuEnergy2020} uses the free energy of the logits directly:

\begin{align*}
    E(x) &= -\log \sum_k \exp\!\bigl(f_k(x)\bigr)
\end{align*}

Higher energy indicates OOD.
Unlike softmax confidence, the energy score is not normalised, so it can distinguish low-density regions from high-density ones.


\section{Evaluation Without Labels}\index{silhouette score}\index{Calinski-Harabasz index}

\marginnote{\newthought{Without ground-truth labels,} internal metrics are the only quantitative feedback we have.}

\newthought{The silhouette score} measures how similar each point is to its own cluster compared to the nearest other cluster:

\begin{align*}
    s(i) &= \frac{b(i) - a(i)}{\max\!\bigl(a(i),\, b(i)\bigr)}
\end{align*}

Averaged over all points, it ranges from $-1$ to $1$; higher is better.

\newthought{The Calinski-Harabasz index} takes a different view, comparing between-cluster dispersion to within-cluster dispersion:

\begin{align*}
    CH &= \frac{SS_B / (K - 1)}{SS_W / (N - K)}
\end{align*}

Higher values indicate tighter, better-separated clusters.

\newthought{Stability analysis} provides a complementary signal.
Run clustering multiple times with different initialisations or subsampled data, then measure how consistently points are assigned to the same cluster.
Stable assignments suggest that the structure is genuine rather than an artefact of the algorithm.


\section{Acting Under Uncertainty: Graceful Degradation}\index{graceful degradation}\index{human handoff}

\marginnote{\newthought{Safe AI} means knowing when to hand off to a human.}

\newthought{Quantifying uncertainty} is only useful if we act on it.
Deployed ML systems must handle the inevitable cases they cannot confidently resolve.
Four strategies compose into a practical safety net.

\newthought{Confidence thresholds:} only act autonomously on predictions above a calibrated confidence level.

\newthought{Human handoff:} route uncertain or high-stakes cases to human reviewers.

\newthought{Fail-safe defaults:} when uncertain, take the conservative action, for example holding a transaction rather than approving it.

\newthought{Monitoring:} track prediction confidence and anomaly scores over time; alert on distribution shift before it causes silent failures.

\section{Examples \& Exercises}\index{examples}\index{exercises}

\newthought{Computing by hand} builds the intuition that no anomaly score can replace.
Step through the arithmetic slowly.

\vspace{1em}

\newthought{Z-score on a sensor reading.} Daily mean temperatures over a week read $\{19, 21, 20, 22, 19, 20, 35\}$\,\textdegree{}C. Compute the Z-score of the last reading and decide whether it crosses the conventional $|z|{>}3$ threshold.

\marginnote{%
\begin{align*}
    \mu &= \tfrac{19+21+20+22+19+20+35}{7} \\
        &= 22.3 \\[4pt]
    \sigma &\approx 5.42 \\[4pt]
    z_7 &= \tfrac{35 - 22.3}{5.42} \\
        &\approx 2.34
\end{align*}%
Below the conventional threshold of $3$, but $35$\,\textdegree{}C{} is clearly outside the typical week's spread. Heavy-tailed or small-sample data routinely produces this kind of false negative; the Z-score is a starting point, not a verdict.}

\newthought{LOF by hand.}
Given five points $x_1{=}(0,0)$, $x_2{=}(1,0)$, $x_3{=}(0,1)$, $x_4{=}(1,1)$, $x_5{=}(5,5)$ with $k{=}2$, compute the 2-distance (the distance to the second-nearest neighbour) for each point and decide which has the highest LOF.

The four cluster points have a $2$-distance of approximately $1.41$ each, since the second nearest neighbour sits at the diagonal of the unit square:
\begin{align*}
    d_2(x_1) &= d(x_1, x_4) \\
             &= \sqrt{1^2 + 1^2} \\
             &= 1.41
\end{align*}

For $x_5{=}(5,5)$, the nearest cluster point is $x_4{=}(1,1)$:
\begin{align*}
    d(x_5, x_4) &= \sqrt{4^2 + 4^2} \\
                &= 5.66
\end{align*}

The second nearest cluster point is either $x_2$ or $x_3$, both at distance $\sqrt{4^2 + 5^2} \approx 6.40$. So $d_2(x_5) \approx 6.40$, more than four times the cluster's $2$-distance. The local reachability density of $x_5$ is correspondingly low while its neighbours' densities stay high, so $\text{LOF}_2(x_5) \gg 1$ and the four cluster points sit at $\text{LOF}_2 \approx 1$.

\marginnote{\newthought{Why $k$-NN distance also catches this.} With one isolated outlier and a single dense cluster, simple $k$-NN distance is enough. LOF earns its keep when two clusters of different density coexist; see Figure~\ref{fig:lof_example}.}

\section{Self-Reflection and Recap}\index{reflection}\index{recap}

\newthought{Self-Reflection} questions to guide your thinking:
\begin{itemize}
    \item What is the difference between aleatoric and epistemic uncertainty, and which one can MC Dropout estimate?
    \item When would you prefer deep ensembles over MC Dropout, and what are the trade-offs?
    \item What distinguishes a point anomaly from a contextual one, and why does the distinction matter for method choice?
    \item Why does the Z-score fail on heavy-tailed or multimodal data, and what alternatives exist?
    \item When does $k$-NN distance outperform LOF, and when does it fail?
    \item Why might an autoencoder trained only on normal data reconstruct an anomaly with low error, and how would you mitigate this?
    \item Why is high softmax confidence not a reliable indicator that an input is in-distribution?
    \item How do you set an anomaly threshold in practice when labelled anomalies are scarce?
    \item Why should a deployed model include a human-handoff mechanism, and how would you decide when to trigger it?
    \item How does the silhouette score complement the Calinski-Harabasz index, and when might both disagree?
\end{itemize}

\newthought{Recap} of Key Concepts:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Aleatoric uncertainty is irreducible noise in the data; epistemic uncertainty reflects the model's ignorance and shrinks with more data. Only the second is actionable.
    \item MC Dropout approximates a posterior over weights by leaving dropout on at test time; deep ensembles do the same with $M$ independently trained models, at a higher training cost but sharper, better-calibrated outputs.
    \item Anomaly detection turns uncertainty into a usable score via statistical, proximity-based, or reconstruction-based methods. The type of anomaly, point, contextual, or collective, determines which family fits.
    \item OOD detection asks whether an input is in the training distribution at all. Softmax confidence is unreliable on far inputs; energy and ODIN exploit the unnormalised logits to do better.
    \item A practical safety net stacks confidence thresholds, human handoff, fail-safe defaults, and distribution-shift monitoring. Each compensates for what the others miss.
\end{itemize}

\marginnote{\newthought{The root of model blindness.} Every method in this chapter inherits its notion of normal from the training data; when that notion is wrong or shifts, the detector degrades silently.}

\newthought{The shared limitation} across MC Dropout, ensembles, anomaly scores, and OOD detectors is that each defines unfamiliar with respect to the training distribution it inherits. If the training set itself contains unlabelled anomalies, or if the notion of normal drifts over time, the detector loses its reference and starts certifying as familiar what should have been flagged. The $9$ that an autoencoder trained on $0$ to $8$ reconstructs as a $7$ is the smallest version of this problem; large pretrained models on shifting web data are the largest.

\newthought{Self-supervised learning} attacks the same blindness from the opposite direction. Instead of asking which inputs are unfamiliar relative to a fixed training set, it builds representations whose structure comes from the data itself, organised by augmentations, masking, or contrastive pairs rather than by labels. Those representations then feed every downstream task, including the clustering of Part~I and the anomaly detection of this chapter, on a single shared backbone.
